The Mother Road's Hidden History

Route 66 has long been cemented in the American imagination as a symbol of freedom, mobility, and masculine adventure. Yet, woven into the neon glow and asphalt stretches is a profound, often overlooked history of queer women seeking sanctuary, community, and identity.

In West Hollywood and its surrounding enclaves, the highway served not just as a thoroughfare, but as a lifeline. From clandestine lesbian bars tucked behind unassuming facades to feminist bookstores that functioned as intellectual hubs, these spaces formed an archipelago of resistance.

The June L. Mazer Lesbian Archives seeks to reclaim this geography. By physically mapping these locations—many of which have been lost to gentrification or time—we ground the ephemeral nature of queer history into the tangible landscape of Southern California.

This project is a living document. It invites you to traverse a Route 66 that existed parallel to the mainstream narrative—a road where women found each other, forged radical politics, and built institutions that continue to shape the LGBTQ+ landscape today.
